## Scoring Dimensions (1–10)

*Rubric calibrated to Nerdy's Autonomous Content Generation System evaluation framework. Quality threshold: 7.0/10 average to be considered publishable.*

| Dimension | What It Measures | Score 1 (Bad) | Score 10 (Excellent) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Is the message immediately understandable? | Confusing, multiple messages competing | Crystal clear single takeaway in <3 seconds |
| **Value Proposition** | Does it communicate a compelling benefit? | Generic/feature-focused ("we have tutors") | Specific, differentiated benefit ("raise your SAT score 200+ points") |
| **Call to Action** | Is the next step clear and compelling? | No CTA or vague ("learn more") | Specific, urgent, low-friction ("Start your free practice test") |
| **Brand Voice** | Does it sound like the brand? | Generic, could be anyone | Distinctly on-brand: empowering, knowledgeable, approachable |
| **Emotional Resonance** | Does it connect emotionally? | Flat, purely rational | Taps into real motivation (parent worry, student ambition, test anxiety) |

**Note on CTA scoring:** The Nerdy framework explicitly defines "Learn more" as a Score 1 (Bad) CTA — the literal example of what NOT to do. Since every single Varsity Tutors ad uses "Learn more" as its button CTA, scores are anchored at 1-2 and only raised to 3 if the body text contains a clear, specific embedded action step (e.g., "Fill out the quick form"). Even the best ads in this set are dragged down by this systemic weakness. This is the single biggest opportunity across the entire ad set.

**Note on score severity:** Scores are calibrated strictly to the Nerdy rubric. A 7.0 average is the minimum publishable threshold. Scores of 10 are reserved for genuinely flawless execution on a given dimension — they should be rare. Most ads in a real library will cluster in the 5-7 range. Ads scoring below 5.0 have fundamental problems that require a complete rewrite, not iteration.

3. **Specific numbers** — Score jumps (360 points, 190 points), timeframes (8 weeks, 16 sessions), and price comparisons ($349/month vs. $1,500+ group) add credibility. The more specific the number, the better the ad scores.
4. **Named case studies** — Sarah (1180 → 1390, Westfield) and Jessica (honor roll → 1420) create narrative proof. The best versions include parent quotes.
5. **Risk reversal** — "Cancel anytime" and "perfect tutor match guarantee" appear in the strongest ads and address the #1 objection (commitment fear).
6. **Competitor positioning** — Khan Academy (free but insufficient), Princeton Review (overpriced groups), and Kaplan ($1,500+ groups) are named. This is bold and effective for the comparison-shopper segment.
7. **Emotional validation** — "You're not imagining it," "You're doing the right thing," and "This is completely fixable" are the tone markers that make the best ads feel like VT, not generic tutoring.

### What Fails: Patterns From the Worst Ads

1. **"Learn more" CTA on every single ad** — This is the most damaging systemic weakness in the entire library. The Nerdy rubric literally uses "learn more" as its example of a Score 1 (Bad) CTA. Every ad in this library uses it. This single issue is costing the average ad score 1.0–1.5 points and likely suppressing CTR across the entire campaign. Replacing with "Get Your Child's Free Assessment," "See Your Child's Score Gap," or "Match With a Tutor Today" is the highest-ROI change available. No other optimization comes close.
2. **Production errors shipped to live** — Ads #13 and #18 have visible BEFORE/AFTER/BRIDGE copywriting framework labels. Ad #21 has auto-generated page metadata in its description field. Ads #20 and #21 have identical headline/description/primary text. These are QA failures, not creative problems — they suggest some ads bypass editorial review entirely.
3. **Landing page copy in social ad placements** — Ads #6, #8, #12, #17, #19 are all 150-200+ words. On mobile, Facebook shows ~125 characters before truncating. The strongest elements (pricing comparisons, testimonials, closing CTAs) are consistently buried below "See more" — invisible to 90%+ of readers. The best ad content in this library has the worst ad delivery.
4. **Feature-only copy with no proof** — The athlete ads (#20, #21) have zero score improvement numbers, zero testimonials, zero digital SAT angle. "We improve their SAT score fast" is a feature claim, not an outcome. These read like brochure text.
5. **Creative concept exhaustion disguised as A/B testing** — 42+ active ads with only ~7 distinct creative concepts. Three nostalgia variants changing only the graduation year. Four GPA/SAT gap ads using the same "3.8 GPA. 1260 SAT" headline. Two identical Khan Academy attacks. This isn't creative testing — it's cannibalizing the same message against itself in Meta's auction and driving up CPMs.
6. **Brand voice violations on competitive ads** — "Stop Wasting Time on Khan Academy" (#5) and "Your Child Deserves Better Than Khan Academy" (#4) violate "confident but not arrogant" and "meet people where they are." These are the two lowest-scoring brand voice dimensions in the library (5/10) and risk alienating comparison shoppers.
7. **Unsubstantiated "2.6x" claim** — This stat appears in 10+ ads with no source, no footnote, no explanation of methodology. It's the kind of claim that educated parents (the core audience) will flag as marketing BS. Either cite the source or remove it.
8. **Missing audience segments** — Nearly every ad speaks to parents. Zero ads target students directly, missing the "stressed students" segment entirely. The athlete angle (a genuinely underserved segment) is promising but so poorly executed (scoring 3.2) that it's worse than not trying.

### Brand Voice Compliance

- ⚠️ "Lead with outcomes, not features" — Strong in the top ads (#1, #2, #11, #12, #16). Completely absent in the athlete ads (#20, #21) which are pure feature copy. Mid-tier ads often start with outcomes but bury them below the fold.
- ❌ "Confident but not arrogant" — This guideline is violated in 2 of the 21 scored ads: "Stop Wasting Time on Khan Academy" (#5, brand voice 5/10) and "Your Child Deserves Better Than Khan Academy" (#4, brand voice 5/10). "We Fix That" (#13) also leans arrogant. These aren't borderline — they're the opposite of what the guideline calls for.
- ✅ "Expert but not elitist" — Generally well-executed. The sports analogies, case studies, and plain language keep it accessible. The diagnostic framing demonstrates expertise without talking down.
- ⚠️ "Meet people where they are" — Excellent for anxious parents. Missing entirely for stressed students (zero student-targeted ads). The athlete angle tries to meet a new segment but fails in execution. The nostalgia ads are a creative concept for meeting parents in their own experience, but are disconnected from the product value prop.

### Overall Calibration Note

The previous version of this document scored too generously. Under strict Nerdy rubric calibration, only **1 of 21 ads** (4.8%) scores above 8.0, and only **8 of 21** (38%) meet the 7.0 publishable threshold. The single biggest factor is the universal "Learn more" CTA, which the rubric explicitly defines as Score 1 (Bad). If Varsity Tutors replaced "Learn more" with specific, compelling CTAs across the library, approximately 5-7 additional ads would cross the 7.0 threshold — roughly doubling the publishable rate from 38% to 60-70% with a single systemic change. The copy quality in ads like #2, #11, and #16 is genuinely excellent; the CTA is the bottleneck.

### The Core Lesson

The jump from the real library's average (6.4) to a 9+ isn't about better copywriting. The ads scoring 7.6-8.0 in the real library already have excellent copy. The jump comes from three structural fixes:

1. **Replace "Learn more" with a real CTA.** This alone would add 1.0-1.5 points to every ad in the library. "Book a Tutor" and "Match With a Top 5% Tutor" are night-and-day improvements.

2. **Cut the word count in half (or more).** The 10/10 ad delivers a complete message in 52 words. The best real ads try to deliver 200+ words and bury their strongest elements below the fold. On mobile, shorter IS better. Every word above ~60 is a word most readers will never see.

3. **Trust the story to sell.** The 10/10 ad doesn't need a feature checklist, a price comparison, three competitor callouts, and an unsourced statistical claim. It needs one parent, one student, one score jump, and one invitation to act. The strongest real ads (#1, #2, #16) already prove this — they just get undermined by length and CTA weakness.
